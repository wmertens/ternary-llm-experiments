  Hypothesis #2 (post-hoc convergence) — plausible but weaker than #1 implies.
  The QAT-from-scratch results don't directly prove distillation can reach the

   100k+ steps with the discreteness baked in. We're starting from a continuous
  teacher whose specific weight values were optimized with continuous

  bounded by how aligned the teacher's distribution is with the ternary lattice.

  Hypothesis #3 (perturbation lets weights migrate AND lower EMA) — correct in 
  principle, but our implementation isn't really doing it. The intent is
  essentially simulated annealing: peaks lock latents into basins, troughs let

  1. We're cycling penalty strength (λ), not latent positions. Variable λ would

  produces about the same histogram as constant λ=3.5e-3.

● Good catch — it's worth explaining cleanly. KL decays by design, baked into
  the soft forward:

  # qlinear.py:155 — soft_ternary
  T_α(w) = c(w) + (1-α)·(w - c(w))

  This is a blend between identity (α=0) and hard ternary (α=1). The derivative
  w.r.t. w is exactly (1-α), so when you backprop KL through the forward to the
  latent, the gradient that arrives at w is (1-α) · ∂KL/∂T. The c(w) branch
  contributes zero (it's piecewise-constant; no STE is used inside
  soft_ternary).

  Why it's designed that way — it's a curriculum:

  ┌──────┬─────────────────────┬─────────────────┐
  │  α   │  Forward behavior   │ Latent receives │
  ├──────┼─────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ 0    │ identity, T=w       │ full KL signal  │
  ├──────┼─────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ 0.5  │ halfway to ternary  │ half KL signal  │
  ├──────┼─────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ 0.95 │ nearly hard ternary │ ~5% KL signal   │
  ├──────┼─────────────────────┼─────────────────┤
  │ 1.0  │ T = c(w) exactly    │ zero KL signal  │
  └──────┴─────────────────────┴─────────────────┘

  Early in training, the latent is free to move to whatever value KL likes
  (continuous-teacher-style learning). As α grows, the forward output becomes
  increasingly dominated by c(w) = {−1, 0, +1} · scale — so the model
  "experiences" the ternary constraint, and the KL gradient progressively stops
  adjusting the latent values themselves. At α=1, the forward is hard ternary
  and the latent receives no KL signal at all — it's been "committed" to
  whatever basin it ended up in.

  This is why the well penalty is the load-bearing force at high α: it's the
  only thing pulling latents toward basin minima once KL has been silenced.
  Without it, latents would just sit wherever they happened to be when KL went
  silent — typically not at exact ±a, 0.

  The user-visible knob: --soft-alpha-max 0.95 caps α below 1.0 to keep some KL
  signal (5%) flowing all the way to the end, otherwise the polish phase
  couldn't adjust latents at all. The hard-rounding only happens in finalize.py
  after the soft stage.
